Developer Evangelist vs Advocate

December 7, 2019 in General Topics

The question pops up from time to time. What is the difference between a Developer Evangelist and Developer Advocate?

I should know, since I've had both titles (working as a Developer Advocate now), but the reality is, that both titles are loosely defined, and what you do very much depends on the company you work for. I think there are some general guidelines though.

Evangelism and advocacy has become a popular way to engage with the end users, the developers and the communities relevant to the product.

Developer Evangelist

The typical Developer Evangelist addresses a broader audience, and presenting at conferences is a common way to introduce developers and communities to the product. The Evangelist also produces content, both as articles, social media posts and guides/tutorials.

You can consider an Evangelist the company's spokesperson, and in a lot of ways, there are similarities between evangelism and marketing. It is important to note, an Evangelist's success is not measured in sales numbers.

Outcome: The Developer Evangelist creates awareness and communities around a product, and introduces the right audience to the product. The goal is to spike interest in the product and get it in the hands of developers.

Developer Advocate

The typical Developer Advocate picks up where the Evangelist left off. Where the Evangelist introduces the product to developers and communities, Advocates typically shows how the product can be used, in the audience's context. The Advocates making sure the developers have the resources they need to become successful with the product. This can be documentation, code samples, developer support etc.

The Advocate work more 2-ways, as the Advocate is both representing the company and the developers/communities. The Developer Advocate's goal is to make both the company and the users (developers) happy, and creating the best possible developer journey (the developer experience from discovery to full implementation in production).

Since the Advocate typically has deeper technical discussions with developers, the Advocate has a good feeling of which obstacles or objections developers have towards the product, and can go back to Engineering and share that knowledge.

Outcome: The Developer Advocate drives developers further down the funnel, and provides what developers need to make an educated decision to implement the product or not. By working more directly with the end users (developers), the Advocate can provide valuable feedback about how the product is being perceived in the community, and how to drive adoption.

Summary

Weather you speak to an Evangelist or an Advocate, they will most likely be able to provide the same. They are both experts in the product, event though their audience typically are different. Communicating the value and use of the product is core, the different lies in how far they are taking you down the path towards actually using the product and integrating the product in their own stack.

Companies choose to have either Evangelists or Advocates, so it's not unusual to have a great deal of overlap between the roles, and the job can easily be something in-between, regardless of the title. A lot of times it comes down to which of the titles the company feels more comfortable with, more than the actual work being done in the role.
